Use: This sutra is used in the following two cases.

(i) To find the roots of a quadratic.

(ii) To factorize higher-degree polynomials, such as those of 3rd, 4th, and 5th degrees.

Key Concept for Chalana-kalanabhyam

Using the sutra, the discriminant (Δ) of a standard quadratic equation ax 2 + bx + c = 0 can be calculated as:

∆ = b 2 - 4ac

Once the discriminant is found, the roots of the quadratic equation can be determined using the calculus-derived formula:

dy
dx
(ax 2 + bx + c) = ±√∆

This process breaks the quadratic equation into two linear equations, which can be solved to find the roots.

Key Concept for Higher-Degree Polynomials

There is a relationship between the factorization of higher-degree polynomials (e.g., 3rd, 4th, or 5th degree) and differential calculus. By differentiating the polynomial, you can systematically identify roots, simplify the expression, and break it into smaller factors.

The ninth sutra of Vedic Mathematics, Chalana-kalanābhyām, is a dynamic technique that draws upon the principles of motion and change concepts, foundational to differential calculus. This sutra demonstrates the deep interconnection between algebra and calculus by enabling the factorization of quadratic and higher-degree polynomials through the use of derivatives and discriminants. It brings out the elegance of solving algebraic expressions by focusing on how quantities vary, thus transforming abstract operations into more intuitive and manageable processes. Additionally, the sutra proves instrumental in factorizing cubic and quadratic polynomials by identifying roots through derivatives and successive simplification.

The utility of this sutra extends seamlessly into scientific contexts, where many relationships are governed by quadratic or exponential functions. For instance, in reaction kinetics, Chalana-kalanābhyām aids in solving the Arrhenius equation, a cornerstone in determining activation energy. Similarly, the sutra proves effective when working with equilibrium expressions that yield quadratic relationships, such as in dissociation reactions or concentration-based problems in chemical equilibrium. By applying the principles of differentiation and factorization, the sutra simplifies complex numerical computations, leading to faster and more accurate analytical insights.
